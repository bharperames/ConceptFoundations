# Pedagogical foundations review

An honest review of the curriculum against the conceptual building blocks that
underpin early critical thinking and understanding: what's covered, what's
partial, what's missing, and where the highest-value additions are.

*Framing:* this synthesizes well-established developmental frameworks — Piaget's
stages, "core knowledge" theory (Spelke, Carey), and the early-math/early-logic
literature on comparison, classification, seriation, and pattern. It is a
principled review, not a citation of specific studies.

---

## 1 · What the app teaches today

| Node | Concept | Cognitive primitive | Coverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Identity | same / different, find the odd one | **discrimination, matching, identity** | ✅ strong |
| 2 Magnitude | big / small | **continuous magnitude** | ✅ (pairwise) |
| 3 Quantity | more / less, subitizing 1–6 | **discrete quantity, subitizing** | ✅ (no counting) |
| 4 Spatial | in/out, on-top, containment | **spatial relations, containment** | ✅ |
| 5 Composition | part → whole assembly | **decomposition / combination** | ✅ |
| 6 Peekaboo | find the hidden one, shell game | **object permanence, working memory** | ✅ |
| Mini: Bubble Pop | tap falling targets | attention, inhibition, motor timing | ➕ (game) |
| Mini: Picture Puzzle | rotate tiles to match a scene | **mental rotation, spatial** | ➕ (game) |

The **method** is a genuine strength and worth stating first:

- **Expose → Contrast → Test → Generalize** with **strict single-variable
  isolation** mirrors how discrimination learning and concept formation actually
  work — introduce the target, contrast against a minimal delta, test, then
  vary the *non-target* dimensions to probe true abstraction.
- **The Generalize stage is unusually good.** Most toddler apps test recognition
  of a fixed visual; testing transfer to new exemplars (shapes → animals) is the
  difference between *concept* and *rote pattern*, and the dashboard even
  measures it (generalization-transfer rate).
- **DDA repair** (step down after repeated struggle) is scaffolding within the
  zone of proximal development — pedagogically sound.

## 2 · The building blocks of early critical thinking

The primitives that later reasoning is built on, roughly in developmental order:

1. **Object** — permanence, individuation, boundaries.
2. **Identity & sameness** — same/different, matching to a sample.
3. **Classification** — grouping by a shared property; "put the same ones together."
4. **Magnitude** — continuous (size) and discrete (number).
5. **Number** — subitizing → 1-to-1 correspondence → counting → cardinality.
6. **Seriation** — ordering three or more by a dimension (small→big).
7. **Space** — relations (in/out, on/under, up/down, near/far), rotation.
8. **Pattern & sequence** — AB / ABC regularities, "what comes next."
9. **Causality** — cause→effect, means→end ("if I do X, Y happens").
10. **Composition** — parts make wholes; decomposition.
11. **Time / order** — first / next / last.
12. **Relational & symbolic** — analogy, one-thing-stands-for-another (later).

Critical thinking is these primitives *combined*: **compare → classify → order →
predict → infer cause**. The app is strong on the first links and thin on the
later ones.

## 3 · Coverage assessment

**Well covered (✅):** identity/discrimination, matching, magnitude (size),
subitizing, more/less, spatial containment & relations, part-whole composition,
object permanence, working memory (shell game), mental rotation (puzzle).

**Partial (◐):**
- **Number** — subitizing yes, but **no counting, no one-to-one correspondence,
  no cardinality.** The jump from "see 3 at a glance" to "count to find how many"
  is a distinct, essential primitive that's absent.
- **Magnitude** — big/small is only **pairwise**; there's no **seriation**
  (arrange 3+ from small to big), which is the step from perception to ordering.
- **Opposites** — same/different, in/out, big/small are opposites but never named
  as the *relation* "opposite."

**Gaps (✗) — named or implied but not built:**
- **Causality.** The project's own inspiration text names "causality" in the
  dependency tree, but no node teaches cause→effect. This is the single biggest
  gap: causal reasoning is the spine of critical thinking, and it's very
  learnable for toddlers (press→light, push→fall, pour→fill).
- **Pattern & sequence.** No AB/ABC pattern completion ("what comes next"). This
  is a cornerstone of early logic and math and is conspicuously missing.
- **Classification / sorting.** Matching-to-sample exists, but never **active
  grouping** — "put all the red ones here." Sorting is the bridge from identity
  to categorization and to logical class-inclusion.
- **Time / order.** No first/next/last, no simple 3-step sequencing.
- **Relational / symbolic.** No analogy or symbol-substitution — appropriately
  later, but worth noting as the ceiling.

## 4 · Structural observations

- **The dependency tree is shallow.** Prereqs are largely linear (Identity gates
  the rest). A richer graph would sequence, e.g., Identity → Classification →
  Seriation, and Quantity → Counting. The vision ("map the entire dependency
  tree of cognitive primitives") is only partly realized.
- **"Generalization" sometimes = re-skin.** Swapping shapes for animals varies
  the exemplar but stays visual/perceptual. True abstraction (transfer across
  *modality* or *relation*) is harder to build and to verify — a place to be
  honest about what the transfer metric proves.
- **Everything is perceptual/concrete** — correct for the age, but there's no
  on-ramp toward representation (a token standing for a thing), which is where
  symbolic thought begins.

## 5 · Highest-value additions (prioritized)

1. **Causality node** *(fills a named gap; high value).* Cause→effect: press a
   button → it lights; push a block → it falls; the itsy-bitsy-spider/pipe idea
   already on the backlog is exactly this (spider on pipe → water flushes it out).
   Teaches "my action produces an effect" — the root of agency and reasoning.
2. **Patterns node** *(cornerstone of logic).* AB then ABC completion: show
   red-blue-red-blue-?, child taps what comes next. Directly builds predictive
   inference.
3. **Sorting / Classification node.** "Put the same ones together" — drag items
   into groups by color/shape/kind. Bridges Identity → categorization.
4. **Seriation** (extend Magnitude). Order 3+ by size, small→big. Perception → order.
5. **Counting / one-to-one** (extend Quantity). Tap each of N, one count per tap,
   land on cardinality. The missing half of number sense.
6. **Sequence / time.** First → next → last: a 3-step order (seed → sprout →
   flower), reusing the drag mechanics.
7. **Opposites** as an explicit relation, tying together the pairs already taught.

Suggested sequencing (dependency-aware):

```
Identity ──► Classification ──► Seriation
   │             │                 │
   ├──► Magnitude ─┘                │
   ├──► Quantity ──► Counting       │
   ├──► Spatial ──► Causality       │
   ├──► Composition                 │
   └──► Peekaboo (permanence)   Patterns / Sequence ◄─┘
```

---

## 6 · Bottom line

- **As a foundation for *comparison and perception*, the app is strong and
  unusually rigorous** — the isolate-and-generalize method and the transfer
  metric are better than most.
- **As a foundation for *reasoning*, it stops one layer short.** The links from
  perception to inference — **causality, pattern, classification, ordering,
  counting** — are where critical thinking actually forms, and four of those five
  are absent or partial.
- **The two additions that would most change the app's ceiling are Causality and
  Patterns.** Both are age-appropriate, both reuse existing mechanics (drag,
  tap, the E→C→T→G loop), and both convert the app from "teaches concepts" to
  "teaches the child to predict and infer."
